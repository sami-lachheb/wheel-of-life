# The Wheel of Life: A Comprehensive Guide

## Origins and Historical Context

The Wheel of Life concept has roots in both ancient Eastern philosophy and modern Western coaching practices.

### Buddhist Bhavachakra

The term "Wheel of Life" originates from the Buddhist concept of Bhavachakra (Sanskrit: भवचक्र; Pāli: bhavacakka), a visual teaching aid and meditation tool symbolically representing saṃsāra (cyclic existence). Found on walls of Tibetan Buddhist temples, the Bhavachakra consists of four concentric circles held by Yama, the lord of Death, with an image of the Buddha pointing to the moon—metaphorically representing liberation from suffering and reincarnation.

The Bhavachakra includes:
- Three poisons (hub): pig (ignorance), snake (aversion), bird/rooster (attachment)
- Karma layer: showing results of positive and negative actions
- Six Paths of samsara
- Twelve Links of dependent origination
- Yama holding the wheel, representing impermanence
- Moon representing liberation from cyclic existence

### Modern Wheel of Life

The modern coaching application was developed by Paul J. Meyer in the 1960s to help people realize their goals. While the wheel today has many different forms and names—including Life Balance Wheel, Coaching Wheel, and Wheel of Success—they share a common purpose: transformation.

## Psychological Foundations

### Basic Psychological Needs

According to Eakman (2016), life moves out of balance "when the fulfillment of basic psychological needs has been thwarted within ongoing patterns of day-to-day occupations" and it causes harm to wellbeing.

Ryan & Deci's Self-Determination Theory (2018) identifies three basic psychological needs:
- **Autonomy**: Feeling in control of one's choices and actions
- **Relatedness**: Feeling connected to and caring for others
- **Competence**: Feeling effective and capable

When these needs are unmet, life satisfaction decreases and stress increases.

### The Balance Paradox

Achieving balance across life dimensions is essential for mental wellbeing. According to Byrne (2005), "an appropriate work–life balance will enhance your overall wellbeing by identifying and aligning the many facets—family, friends, health, work environment, and spirit—of your life."

The paradox: while imbalance can lead to excellence in a specific area, it cannot persist without impacting overall wellbeing and long-term goals.

## The Wheel of Life Framework

### Standard Dimensions

The wheel typically consists of 8-10 categories essential for a fulfilling life. Segment names vary, but the themes are usually similar:

1. **Money & Finances** - Financial security, debt management, income stability
2. **Career & Work** - Job satisfaction, professional growth, work environment
3. **Health & Fitness** - Physical health, exercise, nutrition, sleep
4. **Fun & Recreation** - Leisure activities, hobbies, play
5. **Environment** - Home and work environment quality, organization
6. **Community** - Civic engagement, neighborhood, social contribution
7. **Family & Friends** - Social connections, support network
8. **Partner & Love** - Romantic relationships, intimacy
9. **Personal Growth & Learning** - Education, skill development, curiosity
10. **Spirituality** - Meaning, purpose, values, connection to something greater

### Scoring System

Users score each category on a scale of 1-10 (sometimes 1-5):
- **1**: Not satisfied at all, crisis level
- **5**: Neutral, moderate satisfaction
- **10**: Fully satisfied, ideal state

The scoring creates a visual "wheel" that reveals imbalances—areas that are flourishing versus those needing attention.

### Two Display Formats

**Pie Style**: Pre-drawn diagram with numbers circled or filled in next to each dimension.

**Spider Web Style**: Generated by online coaching tools, with automatic marking based on client input.

## Application in Coaching

### The Six-Step Process

#### Step One: Introduce the Wheel of Life

Provide the client with a printed or digital copy. Explain that the wheel captures a snapshot of how they feel about their life. Review the categories and scoring method.

#### Step Two: Rank the Categories

Ask the client to score each category. This provides an overview of life satisfaction levels.

#### Step Three: Review the Wheel as a Whole

Discuss the overall shape and ask:
- When you look at the shape of the wheel, how do you feel?
- How would you like to change the shape of the inner wheel?
- What surprises you the most?
- What would a score of ten look and feel like?
- Which category would you most like to improve?
- What category would you most like to start with?
- At present, how do you spend time in each area?
- What do you need to improve the score in each area?
- What small steps would have the most significant impact on your satisfaction?
- Could a single action improve more than one area?

#### Step Four: Review Each Section

Explore areas of interest:
- Why does this area need attention?
- What would it take to increase your satisfaction by one score?
- How balanced do you feel in this area of your life?
- Why did you give this score?
- Is there anything missing from this area of your life that may affect your score?
- Is there anything that might add value to this area of your life and change the score?

#### Step Five: Identify Actions

Identify activities for each category that will change satisfaction levels. For longer-term or complex goals, define SMART goals (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound).

#### Step Six: Revisit and Review

Over time, revisit the wheel and re-score to understand changes and actions outstanding. This provides a permanent record of successful transformation and insight into coaching value.

### Socratic Questioning

Use focused, open questions to unpack beliefs about why categories receive their scores and how satisfaction can be improved.

### Mindfulness Integration

A brief mindfulness session can ground the client in the present, ensuring readiness to objectively review scores before identifying next steps.

## Alternative Applications

### Tailored Wheels

The Wheel of Life can be adapted for specific contexts:

- **Work**: Project or promotion focus
- **Education**: Degree or certification preparation
- **Family**: Strengthening marriage or parenting

Choose appropriate segment names (max 10 for manageability), then repeat the six-step process.

### Short-Term Goal Setting

The wheel can capture both current state and future target states, with a green line defining mid-way points to longer-term goals.

## Scientific Support

### Research Findings

Eakman (2016) established that life balance correlates with life satisfaction and fulfillment of basic psychological needs. The Wheel of Life provides a practical method for identifying imbalances that hinder wellbeing.

Byrne (2005) demonstrated that work-life balance enhances overall wellbeing by aligning life's many facets.

### Growth Mindset Integration

Dweck (2017) emphasizes maintaining a growth mindset to sustain transformation. Without it, successful changes may be lost, like a rubber band regaining its original shape after tension is removed.

## Practical Implementation

### Physical vs. Digital

While online versions are valuable for remote coaching, physical copies reduce distraction and allow pen-and-pencil completion. Coaches may also have clients complete digitally before sessions, then discuss during in-person meetings.

### Integration with Coaching Programs

The Wheel of Life can:
- Build trust between client and coach
- Create a picture of overall life satisfaction
- Prioritize and set goals
- Demonstrate coaching success
- Perform check-ins during or outside sessions
- Understand ongoing or temporary issues
- Open sessions with individuals or groups
- Review previously set goals

### Measuring Success

The scoring provides a permanent record of transformation and clear insight into coaching value. Regular re-scoring demonstrates progress and identifies areas requiring continued focus.

## Limitations and Considerations

### Subjectivity

Scores are inherently subjective and may vary based on mood, recent events, or interpretation of categories. Coaches should help clients maintain consistent scoring standards over time.

### Cultural Considerations

The standard dimensions reflect Western values. Cross-cultural adaptation may be necessary for different populations.

### Over-Simplification

Life is complex. The wheel reduces multidimensional experiences to single scores. Coaches should use the tool as a starting point for deeper exploration, not as a complete assessment.

### Static Snapshot

The wheel captures a moment in time. Circumstances and priorities change, and balance is rarely maintained long-term. The tool should be revisited regularly.

## Conclusion

The Wheel of Life remains one of life coaching's most valuable tools. Its strength lies in its flexibility and simplicity. By providing a visual representation of life satisfaction across multiple dimensions, it helps individuals identify imbalances, set meaningful goals, and track progress toward a more balanced, fulfilling existence.

The tool's enduring popularity stems from its ability to make the abstract concept of "life balance" concrete and actionable. When used as part of a broader coaching process that includes Socratic questioning, mindfulness, and SMART goal setting, the Wheel of Life becomes a powerful catalyst for personal transformation.
